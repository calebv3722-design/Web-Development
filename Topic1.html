How Cookies Shape Privacy in the Modern Web

It seems like you go to a site, and the site somehow remembers you, who you are, what you ordered on your cart, what theme you use, or that you have already closed a pop-up that you did not want. Websites accomplish this in a form known as cookies. A cookie is simply a small text that is saved on your browser by a site. Although they have been tiny and modest, these tiny notes are what render the present-day websites appear customized as well as functional. There are cookies that disappear as long as you are on a site and cookies that remain in your computer much longer to make the site familiar with you the next time you visit it. In most situations cookies are very useful, particularly when they remember important issues such as your session such that you do not find yourself having to log in again and again.The privacy issues start when cookies are not utilized because of convenience. There are companies that incorporate third-party cookies into websites and this implies that a person can be being tracked not only on one site but on a large number of sites. These cookies may gradually create an accurate image of your habits: which articles you were reading, what products you were about to purchase, how much time you spend scrolling, which content you like. This is something that most of the people do not witness directly, and thus this makes it a point of concern. It feels awkward to consider the possibility of other businesses you have not even communicated with, constructing a profile of your online actions. Due to this reason, there has been a call to have more privacy protection. The GDPR, as applied in the European Union, mandates websites to obtain explicit consent before installing some cookies on your computer. That is why you see lots of such cookie banners as soon as you open lots of websites. Such laws attempt to provide individuals with greater authority over the kind of data they desire sites to store information about them - as well as the information that they would rather remain confidential. It is a part of a bigger movement to make data collection more open, as opposed to a process that occurs in the background. Security wise cookies must also be treated well. A cookie may have a session ID that identifies you as having been logged in and in case that cookie were to be stolen, somebody would impersonate you. To avoid this, websites should introduce security to cookies that limit their dissemination or access. It is possible to label cookies to only use trusted HTTPS connections, and to block cookies being read by the JavaScript to minimize theft chances. These are technical safeguards, which mitigate the vulnerabilities, but they do not eliminate the privacy concerns related to long-range tracking. This proves that cookies are not wicked per se - they are merely tools. They facilitate websites when utilized in a disciplined manner, and make them easier and more enjoyable. They might destroy privacy in a manner that most individuals are not anticipating when they are used in an untransparent manner. The knowledge of cookies assists us to comprehend why this issue of privacy needs to be taken seriously over the Internet and why contemporary web pages must decide between customization and privacy of user information. ///////// Upon visiting a web site, you forget the extent of the activity that is going on in the background. You can open pages in seconds, your account is automatically logged in, and the products in your cart do not lose their color, and your language preferences are the same as they were yesterday. None of this comes easy--it comes as a result of something known as cookies. A cookie is a small file that is stored in your browser which enables a site to store insignificant amounts of information about you. Although it is tiny and nearly invisible, a cookie is a massive contributor to a feeling of convenience and customization of the internet. Without cookies, you would need to log in each and every time you open a site, your preferences would not be saved, and most of the things that happen everyday just would not work as we expect them to. The complex phase starts when cookies start to go past mere convenience, and start defining your digital identity on the internet. Some of the websites that you visit contain advertisement network, analytics or other social media sites. When their third-party services add their cookies they are able to follow up on your behavior not only in a single site but also in a large number of sites. In the long run, this brings about an unexpectedly rich image of your behavior, what you purchase, what videos you view, what articles you read at night, where you can click, how long you spend on a page, even what mood you may be in, depending on your browsing behavior patterns. What is particularly worrying about this is that the majority of the individuals are not even aware that it happens, and they never gave their express consent in order to have their behavior monitored that closely. This increased awareness has brought the issue of privacy to the limelight. Individuals are posing more difficult questions: Who is receiving this data? How long do they keep it? What are they doing with it? And, most of all,--Why do they ever know so much about me at all? These are the questions that have caused significant reforms across the globe. They started designing regulations in order to ensure that websites are more transparent with regard to the collection of information. This is the reason why cookie banners have become so ubiquitous. Even though a lot of users pass over the Accept All button without thinking, it is the intent of such banners to ensure that individuals have an option on how much information they are comfortable disclosing. These prompts can be seen as a move in the direction of increased user control, although the execution of this can be cumbersome or irritating. Cookies are also vulnerable to cyberattacks in case a person does not treat them properly with regard to security. To take an example, a session cookie, which is used to remember your login, may enable an attacker to impersonate you in case it is stolen. The developers have features such as transmitted cookies, such as only over a secure HTTPS connection or preventing their reading by JavaScript. These security measures do not completely eliminate risks but chances of a person taking over your online identity are minimized. It reminds you that cookies are not only about privacy but they are also closely related to the confidence that you feel when you are on the web. All these demonstrate the complexity that the role of cookies has attained. They facilitate the daily surfing process and are even more entertaining but at the same time, they gripe about the extent to which companies understand us. They are capable of safeguarding your session, at the same time revealing your behavior. They enhance your experience, yet may spoil on your anonymity. It is not the cookies that are good or bad, but just the means. The issue is what their use is intended, and whether users are provided with a real-life control over their personal data. With the increase in population education on privacy, expectations have changed. Users desire customization, and not spying. They desire convenience, without having to lose control of their digital life. Sites which are open with their practices and those which respect the will of the users are gaining more and more credibility as the sites that are secretive in their practices continue to be criticized. In the future, the issue of personalization versus privacy will remain one of the most characteristic aspects of the new web. The initial means of walking that balance more comfortably is to learn about cookies: how they assist us, and how they may cross the line.
